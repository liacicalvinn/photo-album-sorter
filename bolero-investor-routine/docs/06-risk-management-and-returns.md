# 6. Risk management, position sizing & realistic expectations

> **Risk management is the real differentiator.** Position sizing,
> diversification, stop discipline and realistic return expectations matter more
> than stock selection.

## Position sizing

### Swing trades

Risk a fixed **1–2% of total capital per trade** (many pros use ≤1%). On
€35,000, 1% = **€350 of risk**.

```
Position size = risk ÷ (entry − stop)
```

**Example:** entry €50, stop €45 (€5 risk/share) → €350 / €5 = **70 shares
(~€3,500 position).**

Cap total open swing exposure (e.g. ≤20–25% of portfolio) and avoid clustering
correlated names.

### Long-term

Diversify across **~12–25 positions**. Academic and practitioner work suggests
most diversification benefit is captured by ~15–25 stocks; many quality
investors run concentrated books of 20–25 names with single positions around
**4–7%** (some cap any one stock near 10–12% and any one sector near 25%).

For €35k, a practical target is roughly **12–20 positions of ~€1,750–€3,000
each**, so no single mistake is fatal while staying concentrated enough to
matter. **Beware Bolero's per-trade costs** (at least €15 on some non-US European
exchanges) — tiny positions get eaten by fees, so don't over-fragment.

## Diversification & portfolio construction

Consider a **core-satellite** structure: a passive core (a broad world-index
ETF) for the bulk of the money, and a **satellite sleeve** of individual
long-term picks plus a small swing-trading allocation. This anchors you to market
returns while letting you express conviction with limited downside.

**Belgian TOB nuance for the ETF core:** per Curvo, accumulating ETFs with a
Belgium-registered compartment incur TOB of **1.32%** on both purchase and sale,
versus **0.12%** for distributing/non-Belgium-registered funds — an 11-fold
difference. Bolero specifically charges 1.32% on the popular accumulating fund
VWCE — so for the ETF core, **distributing UCITS share classes are usually
cheaper on TOB.**

## Stops & discipline

- Always **pre-define a stop and target for swing trades**.
- For long-term holdings the "stop" is a **broken thesis** (deteriorating ROIC,
  lost moat, balance-sheet stress), not a price wiggle.
- Keep a **trade journal**.
- Don't average down on a swing trade losing on a broken setup; do calmly
  re-evaluate (not panic-sell) a long-term holding on price weakness if the
  thesis is intact.

## Realistic return expectations & the "€35k → €1m" math

Long-run equity returns have historically averaged **~10%/yr nominal (~7%
real)** for US large caps. Forward-looking estimates are more modest: Goldman
Sachs's strategy team (David Kostin, Oct 2024) projected just a **3% annualized
nominal total return for the S&P 500 over the following decade** (range roughly
+7% to −1%), while JPMorgan was nearer ~6% and Goldman noted the average 10-year
forecast across 21 asset managers was ~6%.

Crucially, the average retail investor **underperforms** the index due to
behaviour and costs: DALBAR's *Quantitative Analysis of Investor Behavior* found
the average US equity-fund investor earned ~6.81%/yr over 30 years versus the
S&P 500's ~9.6% — a 2.81-point annual gap — and in 2024 the average equity
investor earned 16.54% vs. the index's 25.02% (an 848-basis-point gap, the 15th
straight year of underperformance).

**So assume ~6–8% net is a realistic planning range unless you prove
otherwise.**

To grow €35,000 to €1,000,000 you need to multiply by ~28.6×. Using compound
math (Rule of 72 for intuition: years-to-double ≈ 72 ÷ return%):

| Scenario | 7%/yr | 10%/yr | 15%/yr (very optimistic) |
|----------|-------|--------|--------------------------|
| **No additional contributions** | ~49–50 yrs | ~35–36 yrs | ~24 yrs |
| **Adding €1,000/month (€12k/yr)** | ~22–23 yrs | ~16 yrs | — |
| **Adding €500/month at 8%/yr** | ~28–30 yrs | — | — |

The dominant lever for most people is **new contributions and time**, not
squeezing a few extra percent of return through risky trading. Targeting 20%+
CAGR sustained for decades is not realistic and usually leads to over-risking and
blow-ups. The goal is reachable, but on a multi-decade timeline driven by
consistent saving + compounding, with active picking aiming to add a modest
increment — not to 10× quickly.
